Q9
Show that the product of two unitary matrices is unitary.
Proof
Let $A$ and $B$ be unitary, so $AA^\dagger = I$ and $BB^\dagger = I$.

We must show $C = AB$ is unitary, i.e., $CC^\dagger = I$. $$CC^\dagger = (AB)(AB)^\dagger = (AB)(B^\dagger A^\dagger)$$ $$ = A(BB^\dagger)A^\dagger = A(I)A^\dagger = AA^\dagger = I \quad \blacksquare$$
Since $CC^\dagger = I$, the product $C = AB$ is unitary.
Q10

Differentiate $x^2=4a(y+a)$: $2x=4ay'$, so $a=\dfrac{x}{2y'}$. Substitute back: $x^2=\dfrac{2x}{y'}\!\left(y+\dfrac{x}{2y'}\right)=\dfrac{2xy}{y'}+\dfrac{x^2}{y'^2}$. $$x^2y'^2=2xyy'+x^2 \implies x^2y'^2-2xyy'-x^2=0$$
Orthogonal trajectories
Replace $y'$ with $-1/y'$: $\dfrac{x^2}{y'^2}+\dfrac{2xy}{y'}-x^2=0$, i.e., $x^2-2xyy'-x^2y'^2=0$, which is identical to the original equation (multiply by $-y'^2/x^2$).
The family is self-orthogonal. $\blacksquare$
